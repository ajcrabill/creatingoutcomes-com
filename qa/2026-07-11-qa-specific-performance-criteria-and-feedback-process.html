--- layout: qa title: "What specific performance criteria and feedback process should a board use to conduct a fair, strategic annual review of the executive director? category: "CEO Evaluation" date: "2026-07-07" ---

Your board should evaluate your executive director against three performance domains: mission alignment (are organizational results moving toward stated goals?), leadership effectiveness (can the executive director mobilize staff and resources toward priorities?), and organizational sustainability (is the organization financially healthy and positioned for the future?). The feedback process should include structured input from board members, key stakeholders served by the organization, and peer leaders, delivered through a written evaluation followed by a face-to-face conversation that focuses on future improvement rather than past judgment.

Each domain needs clear, measurable indicators your board can actually assess. For mission alignment, look at outcomes for those your organization serves—not just activity counts. A hospital trustee examines patient health metrics and access data. A community foundation board member looks at community grant impact and nonprofit grantee health. A nonprofit association board member assesses member engagement and advocacy wins. Avoid vague "leadership" ratings; instead, ask whether the executive director has built a team that can execute strategy.

The feedback process works best when you separate data gathering from evaluation. First, collect input from stakeholders using a consistent survey or interview protocol. Second, have the board chair compile findings without names. Third, share the synthesized feedback with the executive director before the board discussion. This prevents the common mistake of the board voting on impressions rather than evidence.

One honest complication: you may struggle to assess what you don't directly observe. Board members aren't in the organization daily. Address this by relying on stakeholder feedback and documented organizational performance, not your own impressions of the executive director's style. Style matters less than results for those served.

  1. Before the review period starts, agree with your executive director on three to five specific goals tied to your board's strategic priorities. Write them down.
  1. Six months in, send a brief stakeholder pulse survey to those your organization serves. Ask what is improving and what is declining. Share results with the executive director.
  1. At year-end, have the board chair gather written input from each board member using a standard form: mission alignment rating, leadership effectiveness rating, sustainability rating, and one specific example supporting each rating.
  1. Compile board input into a one-page summary. Send to the executive director three days before your in-person review meeting.
  1. In the review meeting, spend the first half discussing future goals and support needs, the second half on the evaluation. End with a clear agreement on two to three priorities for the coming year.
