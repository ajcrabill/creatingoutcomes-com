Clarify Priorities

Our organization's goals are five years old and stale. How do we update them without causing chaos?

June 2026

The fear of "causing chaos" by updating stale goals is real, but it's usually rooted in the wrong concern. Most of the chaos that follows a goal revision doesn't come from the new goals themselves — it comes from how the transition is handled. If the board simply replaces one set of goals with another without explanation or process, staff and management reasonably feel whipsawed. The antidote is transparency about why the goals are changing and continuity in how the organization will be held accountable going forward.

Before you update anything, do a reckoning with the old goals. Ask: which of these did we achieve? Which did we abandon without acknowledging it? Which are simply outdated because the world changed? Being honest about this publicly — including in a board resolution or community communication — actually builds trust rather than undermining it. It shows your organization can learn and adapt, which is far more reassuring than pretending that goals set in a different era are still guiding your work.

The practical transition should include a period of parallel tracking — roughly one to two quarters — where the organization reports against both the old and new goals. This prevents whiplash for staff who have organized programs and workflows around the old targets, and it gives the board a chance to validate that the new goals' measurement systems are working before fully sunsetting the old framework.

What "stale" actually means — and the two types

Goals go stale in two different ways, and they require different responses. The first type is accomplished staleness: you actually made significant progress on the goal and now it represents a baseline, not an aspiration. The right response is to celebrate the achievement, set a maintenance threshold, and move the conversation to the next frontier of improvement.

The second type is irrelevance staleness: the goal no longer reflects what your beneficiaries most need, perhaps because the context changed, leadership changed, or the original goal was never really well-conceived. This requires a more careful conversation, because sunsetting a goal without achieving it can feel like abandonment. The key is to explain what changed in the environment or in your understanding that makes a different goal more appropriate now.

Framing that helps with stakeholders
"Over the past five years, we made meaningful progress on [old goal], moving from X to Y. We're proud of that work. At the same time, our community's needs have shifted and our data now points to [new goal] as the more critical priority for the people we serve. Our new goal reflects what we've learned."

Building in regular review to prevent staleness

The best way to avoid five-year-old stale goals is to treat goal review as an annual board discipline, not an occasional event. Each year, the board should formally assess whether the current goals still reflect the organization's most important accountability to beneficiaries, whether the targets remain appropriately ambitious, and whether the measures are still capturing the right things. Minor recalibrations annually are far less disruptive than wholesale reinventions every five years.
