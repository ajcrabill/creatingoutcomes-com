What should SMART goals look like for a hospital board focused on patient outcomes?

June 2026

SMART goals — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound — are a solid framework, but hospital boards often apply them to the wrong things. They write SMART goals about their own processes (complete a strategic plan by Q3, hold community listening sessions annually) when the only goals that actually matter are about what happens to patients. The board's job is to own the destination; management's job is to figure out how to get there.

For a hospital board, that destination is patient-level. It includes things like mortality rates, readmission rates, infection rates, patient-reported experience scores, and access to care for the community the hospital serves. These are the outcomes your board should be tracking and setting SMART goals around — not the operational metrics that belong in leadership dashboards.

The "specific" and "measurable" elements of SMART are where hospital boards most often go wrong. Saying "improve patient safety" is neither specific nor measurable. Saying "reduce central-line-associated bloodstream infections (CLABSI) to fewer than 0.5 per 1,000 catheter days by December 31" is both. The level of specificity matters enormously — it determines whether your board can actually hold leadership accountable or is just expressing a sentiment.

What strong hospital board SMART goals look like

Good hospital board goals are outcome-level, patient-facing, and time-bounded with a clear baseline. They reference publicly available benchmarks where possible — national rates, Leapfrog scores, CMS star ratings — because that contextualizes what "achievable" actually means and protects the board from being sandbagged with artificially conservative targets.

Before (weak)
"Enhance the patient experience and work toward reducing preventable harm across our system."
After (SMART)
"By December 31 of this fiscal year: (1) achieve a Hospital Consumer Assessment of Healthcare Providers and Systems (HCAHPS) overall rating of 75th percentile or above among peer hospitals; (2) reduce 30-day all-cause readmission rate to below 14%; (3) achieve zero serious safety events in the surgical suite."

Keeping the board out of operations while staying focused on patients

One tension hospital boards face is the fear that setting specific clinical goals means micromanaging the CMO or CNO. It doesn't — as long as the board owns the "what" and gives leadership full authority over the "how." Your board can declare that readmissions must drop to 14% without dictating which care coordination program or transition protocol achieves it. That's not micromanagement; that's governance.

The board should also set equity-explicit goals. Aggregate hospital-wide rates can mask stark disparities by race, insurance status, or zip code. A SMART goal that specifies outcomes for the full patient population — including the most vulnerable — forces your health system to pursue equitable care rather than optimizing for the easiest-to-serve patients. That's the kind of board-level priority-setting that only the board can do, and it's exactly where SMART goals earn their keep.
