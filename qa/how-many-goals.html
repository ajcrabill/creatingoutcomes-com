Clarify Priorities

How many outcome goals should a governing board have?

June 2026

The answer is almost certainly fewer than your board currently has. Most governing boards carry between five and fifteen "strategic priorities" that function more as a list of aspirations than a genuine set of accountable outcomes. Our recommendation: aim for three to five outcome goals at the board level, and treat that ceiling as a discipline, not a suggestion.

Three to five is not an arbitrary number. It reflects what boards can realistically govern — meaning track, discuss meaningfully, and hold leadership accountable for — within the time available at quarterly or monthly meetings. When the list grows beyond five, the board's attention necessarily becomes superficial. Each goal gets three minutes instead of thirty. Trend data gets glanced at rather than interrogated. Accountability becomes ceremonial.

There's also a signal value to a short list. When a board has three outcome goals, everyone in the organization knows exactly what matters most. The CEO can make resource allocation decisions aligned to those priorities. Staff can understand how their work connects to what the board cares about. Funders and stakeholders can see a clear theory of impact. A list of twelve goals sends none of those signals — it says everything is equally important, which means nothing is truly prioritized.

What counts toward the goal count

Count only goals that meet all three criteria: they describe an outcome for your beneficiaries (not a process or activity), they are measurable with specific data, and they are time-bound. Aspirational statements that fail any of these tests are not goals — they're values or intentions, and they belong in your mission statement or your strategic narrative, not your governance goal set.

This counts as a goal
"By June 30, 2027, 85% of clients completing our workforce program will be employed in their target sector within 90 days of completion, up from 68% today."
This does not count as a goal
"Strengthen community partnerships and expand our regional footprint to serve more people in need."

When three feels too few

Boards sometimes resist a short goal list because they fear it implies that everything else is unimportant. It doesn't. A board with three outcome goals still has a CEO running dozens of programs and initiatives — the board is simply focused on the three outcomes that matter most, rather than governing everything simultaneously, which is impossible.

If your organization's mission genuinely spans multiple distinct populations or service lines, you might need goals at the sub-mission level — but even then, each program or division probably needs no more than two or three outcome measures at the board's level of oversight. The discipline of a short goal list is the discipline of leadership: saying clearly what you are most responsible for, and being willing to be judged on it.
