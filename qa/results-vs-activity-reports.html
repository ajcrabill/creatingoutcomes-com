Our CEO reports on activities completed. How do we redirect toward outcomes achieved?

June 2026

A CEO who reports primarily on activities isn't hiding anything — they're reporting what they're being asked to report, even if no one explicitly asked for it. When a board receives activity updates without pushback, it signals that activity updates are what the board wants. Changing this pattern starts with the board changing what it asks for, not with hoping the CEO spontaneously starts reporting differently.

The most direct approach is to change the reporting template. Work with your CEO to define two or three outcome indicators that the board expects to see at every meeting — tied directly to the organization's goals for beneficiaries. These become non-negotiable agenda items. Activities can still be covered, but they move to an appendix or a consent agenda item, not the main event. What the board spends time discussing in meetings is what the organization learns to prioritize.

Before you do that, though, make sure the outcome indicators you're asking for are actually measurable and that your CEO has the data infrastructure to track them. Demanding outcome data your organization doesn't collect yet will produce one of two bad results: manufactured numbers that look like outcomes but aren't, or a CEO who feels set up to fail. The right sequence is to define the outcomes together, identify how you'll measure them, build the tracking, and then require the reporting.

Ask different questions during the meeting

Board culture changes through board behavior, not memos. If your CEO presents activity data and every board member nods along without asking outcome questions, the message is clear: activities are sufficient. Change that by making outcome questions a regular part of your meeting dynamic — not as a gotcha, but as genuine curiosity about mission progress.

Redirecting questions to ask during reports

"Thanks for the program update. What do we know about whether participants are better off as a result of those activities?"

"You mentioned we ran 14 workshops this quarter. Do we have any data on skill retention or behavior change among participants?"

"I'm following the activity numbers — can you help us connect those to where we stand on our goal of [specific outcome]?"

These questions aren't adversarial — they're the questions a board focused on mission is supposed to ask. Over time, a CEO who regularly hears these questions will begin building the answers into their reports proactively. The board's curiosity reshapes what gets prepared.

Distinguish between what you want reported and what you want managed

One reason CEOs default to activity reporting is that outcomes often take longer to measure than the reporting cycle. A quarterly board meeting may not align with the timeline of a meaningful outcome change — especially in education, health, housing, or long-term economic mobility. In those cases, your CEO may be reporting activities because that's genuinely what changed this quarter, and the outcomes won't be visible for months or years.

The solution is to build a reporting structure that distinguishes between leading indicators (early signals that outcomes are on track) and lagging indicators (the actual outcome data, reported less frequently). A hospital board might review patient experience scores monthly as a leading indicator of care quality outcomes measured annually. A workforce development board might see weekly enrollment and engagement rates as leading indicators of six-month employment outcomes. Ask your CEO to identify which leading indicators reliably predict the outcomes you care about, then build those into the regular reporting cycle.

This approach respects the reality of outcome timelines while still keeping the board focused on something other than activity counts. It also gives your CEO something useful to report each meeting — forward-looking signal, not just backward-looking activity — which makes the shift from activity to outcome reporting feel less like a loss and more like an upgrade.
